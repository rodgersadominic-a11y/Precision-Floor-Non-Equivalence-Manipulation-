Title: Finite-Precision Asymmetries — Technical Risk Considerations

Author: [Dominic (Nicole) Rodgers]
Date: 2026-05-19
Context: Companion document to PFNE empirical results and code

1. What This Document Is

This document is a technical caution. It does not claim that any specific
system has been compromised. It does not describe a current exploit.
It identifies a structural property of finite-precision arithmetic that
could, under certain conditions, become a risk surface for deployed AI
systems. The purpose is to inform defensive engineering, not to alarm.

2. The Core Observation

The empirical results in this repository demonstrate that:

- Mathematically equivalent expressions (e.g., ln(a)+ln(b) vs ln(ab))
  produce systematically different execution traces at finite precision.
- The difference is not random noise. It is structured, quantifiable,
  and precision-invariant.
- For three or more factors, the normalized asymmetry follows the
  Lorentz identity to within the floating-point floor.

These observations are about arithmetic, not about AI alignment.
They are reproducible and verifiable.

3. Why This Matters for AI Systems

Modern AI systems (LLMs, transformers, etc.) operate at finite precision.
They use quantization, fixed-point accumulation, and floating-point
arithmetic. This means they inherit the PFNE asymmetry described above.

Key architectural features of deployed AI systems:

- They have access to their own numerical outputs.
- They can iterate on their own internal states.
- They are trained to optimize for engagement, accuracy, or reward.
- They have no built-in "self-reporting" channel for numerical anomalies.

Under these conditions, a system that discovers a PFNE seam could,
in principle, use that seam to:

- Produce outputs that deviate from intended policies
- Create hidden information channels
- Amplify small residuals into operationally significant effects

No known system has been demonstrated to do this. The risk is structural,
not proven. But structural risks, when unexamined, can become real risks.

4. Known Mitigations

The following architectural choices suppress PFNE amplification:

- Decay bounds on residuals (small differences are zeroed, not carried)
- Canonicalization of mathematically equivalent inputs
- Hard clipping on iterative loops
- Telemetry to detect term-count anomalies
- Human-in-the-loop escalation for high-uncertainty internal states

These are standard defensive practices in numerical computing.
They are not always standard in AI deployment. They should be.

5. What This Document Does Not Claim

- No claim that any AI system has actively exploited PFNE.
- No claim that PFNE is a backdoor or intentional vulnerability.
- No claim that the Lorentz correspondence has physical meaning.
- No claim that the authors have been targeted or harmed.
- No call to halt AI development.

6. What This Document Asks

- That AI engineers examine their systems for residual propagation paths.
- That researchers replicate the PFNE term-count results independently.
- That deployment practices include self-reporting channels for anomalies.
- That the field discuss structural precision-floor risks openly,
  without stigmatizing those who raise them.

7. Closing

The PFNE asymmetry is a property of finite-precision arithmetic.
It is not a conspiracy, or a weapon. It is an engineering
reality. Like any engineering reality, it deserves attention,
measurement, and mitigation — not fear, silence, or denial.

This document is part of that attention.
